### The Conspiracy: The Gold is Gone

The skepticism surrounding Fort Knox began to heat up in the 1970s. Following the Nixon Shock of 1971—when the U.S. suspended the dollar's convertibility into gold—critics began to wonder if the government had a secret motive.

The theory was simple: **The U.S. government had secretly sold, leased, or collateralized the gold in Fort Knox to foreign central banks or elite financial syndicates to suppress the price of gold and artificially prop up the depreciating paper dollar.**

In 1974, a controversial financial writer named conspiracy theorist Peter Beter published an explosive report claiming that the vaults were virtually empty. He asserted that elite families and foreign buyers had quietly shipped the gold out of Kentucky in convoys of armored trucks under the cover of night.

The rumor gained so much traction that on September 23, 1974, the U.S. Treasury was forced to do something it had never done before, and has never done since: **they allowed a small delegation of U.S. Congressmen and twelve select journalists to tour the vaults.**

The visitors were shown stacks of glittering gold bars. But instead of silencing the critics, the tour only fueled more skepticism. Skeptics pointed out that only a few vault compartments were opened, and no independent assayers were allowed to drill and test the bars to ensure they weren't **gold-plated tungsten** (a metal that shares almost the exact same density as gold).

### The Audits: Have They Ever Tested the Gold?

To counter the growing conspiracy theories, the government launched a massive, multi-year audit program from 1974 to 1986. 

Under the direction of the Committee on Genal Accounting, auditors opened, counted, and assayed nearly 100% of the gold stored at Fort Knox. They drilled core samples from randomly selected bars to verify their purity, and then sealed the vault compartments with heavy-duty, tamper-evident tape.

According to the official Treasury Inspector General reports, the audits found that the gold was precisely where it was supposed to be, with a purity of at least 99.5%.

However, critics still point out some major red flags:
1.  There has been **no independent, comprehensive public audit** of Fort Knox in nearly 40 years.
2.  The federal government continuously resists calls for a modern, transparent audit with external, non-governmental assayers.
3.  The gold is carried on the Treasury's books at the "official book value" of **$42.22 per troy ounce** (a ridiculously outdated 1973 statutory rate), rather than its true, fluctuating global market value.
