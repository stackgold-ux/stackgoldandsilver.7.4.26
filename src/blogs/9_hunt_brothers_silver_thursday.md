# The Day the Silver Market Broke: The Audacious Hunt Brothers Corner of 1980

*How two Texas oil billionaires nearly cornered the global silver supply, drove prices up 800%, and triggered the financial panic of "Silver Thursday."*

---

In the early morning hours of January 1980, the global financial system was on the verge of heart failure. 

In New York, London, and Zurich, precious metals trading desks were in absolute chaos. The price of silver, which had quietly hovered around **$6.00 per ounce** just a year prior, had exploded to an unfathomable **$50.36 per ounce**. 

Jewelry manufacturers were shutting down, coin shops were running out of cash, and central banks were watching in horror.

Behind this explosive surge were two men: **Nelson Bunker Hunt** and **William Herbert Hunt**. These brothers—sons of the legendary Texas oil tycoon H.L. Hunt—were executing the most audacious, high-stakes market corner in modern history. 

This is the story of the day the silver market broke, the regulatory crackdown that followed, and the eternal lesson it holds for physical stackers today.

---

### The Motivation: A Hatred of Paper Money

To understand why the Hunt brothers did what they did, you have to understand the era. 

The 1970s was a decade of economic misery. Following President Richard Nixon’s decision to sever the U.S. dollar's last link to gold in 1971, inflation was spiraling out of control. The dollar was rapidly losing its purchasing power, and the Hunts—billionaires who owned vast oil reserves—realized that paper wealth was a dangerous illusion.

As Bunker Hunt famously stated: **"Any damn fool can run a printing press."**

Seeking a safe haven to anchor their family’s massive fortune, the Hunts looked at gold. However, in the early 1970s, private ownership of gold bullion was still illegal for U.S. citizens (a restriction dating back to FDR’s executive order in 1933). 

So, the Hunts turned their attention to the other king of hard money: **Silver**.

---

### The Strategy: Taking Physical Delivery

The Hunts didn't want to just trade paper "futures" contracts on Wall Street. They wanted the real thing. 

Starting in 1973, the Hunts began buying silver futures contracts on the COMEX exchange. But instead of settling those contracts in cash at expiration (which is what 99% of Wall Street traders do), the Hunts did something that shocked the financial world: **They demanded physical delivery.**

They loaded millions of ounces of .999 fine silver bars onto specially chartered, heavily armed airplanes and flew them to ultra-secure private vaults in Switzerland. 

By 1979, alongside wealthy partners from Saudi Arabia, the Hunt brothers had quietly accumulated over **200 million ounces of physical silver**. This represented nearly **half of the world's entire deliverable commercial supply**. 

As the physical supply vanished into the Hunts' vaults, the basic laws of supply and demand took over. The price of silver skyrocketed:
*   *January 1979:* **$6.00 / oz**
*   *September 1979:* **$16.00 / oz**
*   *January 1980:* **$50.36 / oz**

The Hunts' paper silver fortune was valued at over $10 billion, making them the richest men on earth.

---

### The Empire Strikes Back: "Liquidation Only"

The global financial establishment was backed into a corner. If the Hunts demanded physical delivery on their remaining contracts, the COMEX exchange would default—they literally did not have the physical silver to fulfill the orders.

So, the exchanges and federal regulators decided to change the rules of the game mid-match.

In early 1980, the COMEX board of governors implemented a unprecedented rule change: **"Liquidation-Only" trading.**
1.  No new "buy" orders were allowed. Traders could only sell.
2.  Margin requirements were aggressively raised. If you wanted to hold a silver contract, you had to instantly put up massive amounts of cash.

This artificial rule change broke the buying momentum. Silver prices plateaued and began to slide. Because the Hunts had leveraged their oil assets to buy more silver contracts, they were hit with massive "margin calls"—demands from banks to deposit hundreds of millions of dollars in cash to cover their positions.

---

### March 27, 1980: Silver Thursday

The breaking point arrived on Thursday, March 27, 1980. 

Unable to meet a $100 million margin call, the Hunts’ brokerage firm began liquidating their positions, dumping millions of ounces of silver onto the market. 

Panic rippled through Wall Street. The price of silver collapsed in a single day, crashing from **$21.62 down to $10.80**. The stock market plummeted, brokerage houses teetered on the edge of bankruptcy, and the Hunts’ paper fortune was virtually wiped out overnight. They were forced to declare bankruptcy and were eventually fined over $130 million for market manipulation.
